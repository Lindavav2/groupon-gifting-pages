§ 01

Four cohorts, one funnel

The gifting journey creates four distinct user types. Two are on the demand side — gifters who purchase and recipients who redeem. Whether a recipient places a post-redemption order determines whether they join the most valuable cohort in Groupon's consumer base, or disappear from the data entirely.

The conversion gap
Of the 208,965 users who redeemed a gift voucher, only 39,380 (19%) went on to make a standard purchase. The remaining 169,585 — redeemed and churned — represent the largest addressable opportunity in the gifting funnel.

§ 02

Redeeming a voucher unlocks something

When a gift recipient redeems their voucher, the experience does something no standard acquisition channel reliably does: it gets a new user into a merchant experience with zero purchase friction. The data shows these users come back — and they come back fast. 52% repurchase within 90 days of redemption, at 5.7× the order cadence of a standard first-time buyer.

§ 03

Converted recipients are worth 6× a standard buyer

The LTV gap is large enough to change how you think about acquisition economics. A converted recipient is worth $54.54 over 12 months — not because they spend more per order, but because they order more than twice as often as a gifter and nearly six times as often as a standard buyer. The driver is frequency, not price.

AOV comparison — Method A vs Method B

There are two valid ways to define AOV for gifting cohorts. Method B — proposed by Nada Moravcikova — weights gifter AOV by the share of their spend that is gift-attributed. Only the Gifter cohort is materially affected. Converted recipients are identical under both methods because their post-redemption orders carry no gift-flagged NOB.

AOV by cohort
Method A vs Method B — which cohorts change?

Only the Gifter bar changes between methods. Under Method B, applying the ~65% gift NOB share reduces gifter AOV from $28.02 to $18.21. All other cohorts are identical — their post-entry orders carry no gift attribution.

§ 04

Within gifters, occasion frequency drives value

The gifter cohort looks homogenous from the outside, but it isn't. The 10% who give three or more times a year are worth nearly as much as a converted recipient. The 71% who give once are barely above the standard buyer baseline. Gifting frequency — not AOV — is the variable worth optimizing.

§ 05

Converted recipients stay. Gifters occasion-lapse.

The LTV gap between cohorts is durable, not a short-term spike. At month 12, 38% of converted recipients are still active — nearly 5× the rate of standard buyers. Gifter retention is stronger than standard in the first 3 months, then converges. This confirms gifting is occasion-driven: gifters return when they have someone to buy for, not out of habitual platform use.

§ 06

Key Observations

Six findings ordered by strategic implication. Click any card to reveal the recommended action.

01
The conversion moment is worth more than the gift itself
A converted recipient generates $54.54 in year-one LTV. The gift that brought them in may have cost $25–$30. The post-conversion value far exceeds the acquisition signal — and it compounds through repeat ordering at 5.7× the standard buyer cadence.
→ Optimize the experience at and immediately after redemption. Post-redemption conversion is the highest-ROI intervention in the gifting funnel.
Click to see the implication →
02
The frequency gap is the real story — not AOV
Converted recipients have a lower AOV than gifters ($25.81 vs $28.02). Their 6.4× LTV advantage is entirely explained by order frequency: 2.15 orders/year vs 0.87 for gifters, 0.38 for standard buyers. AOV optimization is the wrong lever here.
→ LTV improvement comes from driving repeat visits and repeat purchases, not basket-size optimization.
Click to see the implication →
03
19% conversion rate is both the problem and the opportunity
Only 19% of redeemed recipients become buyers. The other 81% — 169,585 users — had a brand experience, redeemed their voucher, and disappeared. At converted recipient LTV value, moving just 5% of non-converters would add ~$32M to cohort 12-month value.
→ Post-redemption nurture (in-app prompt, email at redemption) is the single largest untapped lever in the gifting funnel.
Click to see the implication →
04
Under either AOV method, cohort rankings don't change
Method B reduces gifter LTV from $23.52 to ~$15.84 — a 33% drop. But gifters remain 1.85× more valuable than standard buyers. The converted recipient vs gifter gap widens under Method B (3.4× vs 2.3×), reinforcing the conversion narrative.
→ The AOV methodology debate doesn't change the strategic conclusions. Both methods point to the same priorities.
Click to see the implication →
05
Repeat gifters are a hidden high-value segment
3×+ gifters (10% of the gifter base) generate $42 LTV — 1.79× the overall gifter average and approaching converted recipient territory. These are seasonal occasion buyers: Q4, Valentine's Day, Mother's Day. Currently treated identically to one-time gifters in CRM.
→ Identify and re-engage gifters before the next seasonal gift window. A pre-occasion message to prior gifters could meaningfully improve frequency and cohort LTV.
Click to see the implication →
06
Gifter retention is occasion-driven, not platform-driven
Gifters drop from 32% retention at 3 months to 15% at 12 months — steeper than standard buyers after the initial period. This confirms gifting is triggered by external occasions, not platform affinity. Without an occasion trigger, gifters don't self-activate.
→ Gifter retention programs need to map to gift occasions, not engagement metrics. A calendar-aware CRM approach is more effective than generic re-engagement.
Click to see the implication →

Methodology & Caveats

LTV formula (both methods)
LTV 12m = AOV × (Total valid orders in 12m ÷ Cohort size)
Source: v_fact_rt_financial_metrics · filter: action = 'capture' · NOB in USD · 12-month window from each user's cohort entry date
Method A — BI Metrics standard (Confluence 82283233304)
AOV = Total NOB ÷ Count of valid orders (NOB > 0)
Applied uniformly. Confirmed by Nada Moravcikova — note: some reports use M1VFM instead of NOB; verify with report owner if comparing across dashboards.
Method B — Gift-share-weighted AOV (Nada Moravcikova, Gifting KPI Summary)
Gift AOV = Total AOV × (Gift NOB ÷ Total NOB)
Gift NOB = sum of NOB where intend_as_gift = 1 · Affects Gifter cohort only · All other cohorts carry no gift-flagged NOB after their entry event
